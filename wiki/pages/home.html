In 1969, Dr. Baruch Blumberg developed the first vaccine against hepatitis B — a discovery that won him the Nobel Prize and has since saved millions of lives.

A vaccine exists and it works. But what about the 254 million people already living with chronic hepatitis B? Current treatments can control the virus. But they don’t cure it. The reason?

A molecular structure called cccDNA — a tiny circle of viral DNA that hides deep inside liver cells and keeps the infection alive, even decades later.

WE ARE ENGINEERING A SYSTEM TO CHANGE THAT
We are building a dual-CRISPR system that targets cccDNA with unprecedented precision — without cutting the DNA. By using two engineered guide RNAs that lock together like molecular puzzle pieces, we bring two dCas proteins to key regulatory regions of the virus’s genetic reservoir — the cccDNA.

This precise assembly enables us to epigenetically repress viral transcription at its root — blocking replication without damaging the host genome.
